VPs and how to use them

Hi hi, welcome to the sixth post on this blog. This week, I want to try something different, and talk about a game design topic instead of something technical. The main focus of this blog will still be technical stuff (and code you can take and put in your mods), but I want to broaden the type of topics I talk about a bit. It’s unavoidable that this will be a bit more subjective than the coding guides, so take this as a, hopefully still useful, opinion, and not as something that should be taken as gospel. Anyway, enough introduction, let’s talk about the topic of the day: VPs and how to best use them. Note, I’m not going to be talking about VP switching or the midgame VP picker this time.

Choosing a VP alongside the candidate you play has been a feature of the game since the very beginning - even the earliest Dan Bryan scenarios have them. Even before the game proper starts, they give the player some agency. Picking them can both change the ideology of the campaign (by shifting the starting position of your issue scores), give a boost in their home state (though that boost is quite small) and give you access to completely different questions (every VP gets its own code 2, which means the questions, answers and effects can differ dramatically without needing to use CYOA).

The first two point I mentioned are pretty clear, so I won’t go into much more detail here - just know that how much influence your VP has on your issue scores is determined by global_parameter_json[0].fields.running_mate_issue_weight, their actual stances by campaignTrail_temp.running_mate_issue_score_json and their home state by running_mate_state_id. Just log them to the browser console to see how they look, then adjust them for your mods. What’s the more interesting question in my opinion is how much should they influence how your campaign plays? And if you’re making a mod for an election without VPs, what should you put in that slot? Spoilers: A lot, and there are a few different options.

The classic question for your VP - but you can do much more than that!

Back when there was just The Campaign Trail and no New Campaign Trail, VPs didn’t matter all that much. In the earliest scenarios, you didn’t even get a question devoted to them, and in the later scenarios, you usually only got that one “You chose person X as VP, why?”, and then you talk about their competence or their home region. This is fine if you’re making a game that’s mostly for informational purposes and you use the VP slot mostly to show players what people were considered as VPs historically. It doesn’t really make the gameplay a lot more interesting, though. The VPs might make a small difference at the margins, but overall, you’re playing the game the same way no matter who you choose as VP.

Modding gave creators the ability to change almost everything about the game, so it’s no surprise that the way VPs are used also got changed. One thing a lot of early mods did was making the list of VPs comparatively huge - Dan Bryan gave us between three and five potential VPs, while some mods upped that number closer to ten. In my opinion, that wasn’t really an improvement when the VPs didn’t matter more than in an original scenario. What’s the point of having ten VPs if the game plays the same with all of them? For historical mods, this was even counterproductive. With a list of five, you would probably read all their bios and learn which candidates were seriously considered. With ten, you’d probably give up at some point, just select someone and walk away not knowing who was actually a plausible choice and who was a more far-fetched one just thrown in by the modmaker. So, my first lesson here would be - don’t overstuff the VP menu for no reason. If a VP plays basically the same as another one and wasn’t on a historical shortlist, they don’t make your mod better.

Now that’s really cool! And it changes a lot - suddenly, choosing a different VP actually changes the game in a meaningful way, so you have a good reason to go back and play the scenario with someone else. In my opinion, making good use of VPs is a great way to make your mod more varied and replayable. My (very rough) guideline would be that a VP in a 25 question mode should change something in at least five questions if you want the VP choice to feel impactful - be it the whole question, unique answers, or different feedback and effects for answers that look the same. You can get away with having them change fewer questions by making the changes bigger, but I think spreading the effects out over more questions is more elegant.

So, what are some cool things you can do with VPs? I’ll list some examples here for (US presidential) mods that use VPs in cool ways, so you can get some inspiration. Of course, don’t try to do everything at once in a single mod, but putting something like this in your mod can help it stand out without having to use more complicated technology:

  • Put some states or whole regions into play only with a certain VP, but sacrifice some other regions in return. This will almost make your mod feel like a different game with that VP, since you totally have to change strategies. Examples: Hyperion 1884, 1964 Draft Lodge

    • A note of caution here though: VPs putting different areas into play and allowing for different strategies is good overall, but you should be mindful of it making sense. Just adding a VP that completely changes the map without it making a lot of sense doesn't make your mod better, it makes it look like you don't know what you're doing. Varied gameplay is a good thing, but don't completely sacrifice realism for it. Also, the VP helping you in the North while sacrificing most of the South in pre-Civil Rights mods is a classic (and can make sense when done well), but can also feel overdone and a bit stale.
  • Make the campaign partly about what your VP is up to and the consequences they and you) face for their actions. Examples: 1972d plays this straight, 2000 Palpatine does this in a hilarious way
  • Make it harder to win with a certain pick, but satisfying (by giving a better ending or achieving a representation milestone early). Examples: 1952Red (with Helen Douglas), 1968Romney (with Edward Brooke)

The next few examples are a bit more complicated and require some more technology, but can be very worthwhile:

  • Have a VP that is very powerful and gives you strong effects, but needs you to play a certain way to not leave the ticket (Kaptur as Bernie’s VP in 2016-4way redux)
  • Lock some answers (but keep them visible), only “unlocking” them with the fitting VP (a cool tech that 4352 BCE: Cold Famine uses - I might talk about how to implement this in the future)
  • Have a whole alternate history novella for your players to read if they win all states with a certain VP (yeah… I don’t know if another mod will manage to pull this off properly, but it’s so great with Butler in 1864)

These are just some ideas to get you inspired - I’m sure there are cool things to do with VPs that no one has done yet! And remember - a mod that has just three VPs but does interesting things with each of them is more replayable than a mod that has a huge list, but they all more or less play the same.

Now, what about situations in which there isn’t even a VP - like in international or statewide elections. I see some options here that are used by different mods and all work well:

  • Basically, ignore the slot. Put a party or an attorney general or something in there and give the player just one option. In general, for US presidential mods also, a mod doesn’t need different VPs to be fun.
  • Switch it so that the candidate is a party and the VP is their leader. Then, you can have different leaders in the VP slot like 2025NL and 2021DE. Same thing as above applies here, and even moreso: The leaders should play differently to justify their inclusion.
  • Instead of using a party or a person, use a strategy, like the campaign styles in 2017UK. This was a brilliant idea by that mod - the campaign styles serve a similar purpose as the VPs, by giving you a glimpse of what strategies a party could have chosen and changing the campaign to reflect that. You know what I’m gonna say next: Make them different enough to make their inclusion worthwhile!
  • Another idea I toyed with at a point was to use an advisor in this slot that helps you with some questions, like the VPs in the End of History mod.

As you see, even in mods that don’t even have a VP you can still do interesting stuff with that slot if you want!

In conclusion, VPs are a core feature of the game that’s sometimes overlooked, but can really help at making mods feel distinct and be replayable.
